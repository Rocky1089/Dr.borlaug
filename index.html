Dr. Norman Borlaug

The man who save a billion lives
DR. Norman Borloug third from the left,trains biologists in mexico on how to increase wheat yield part of his life-long war on hunger.

Here is a time line of Dr. Norman Borlaug's life;
  • 1914- Born in cresco,lowa
  • 1933- leaves his family's farm to attend the university of minnescea, thanks to a Depression era program known as the "National Youth Administration"
  • 1935- Has to stop school and save up more money, Works in the civilian conservation corps, helping starving Americans. "I saw how food changed them",he said,"All of this left scar on me."
  • 1937- finihes university and takes a job in the US Forestry service
  • 1938- Marries wife of 69 years Magret Gibson. Get laid off due to budget cuts. inspire by Elvin charles stakman, he return to school under stakman, who teach him about breeding pest-resisdent plants.
  • 1941- Tries to enroll in the military after the pearl Harbor attack, but is rejected.Instead, the military asked his lab to work on waterproof glue, DDT to control malarial,distinfections, and other applied science.
  • 1942- Receive a Ph.D. in genestucs and pathology
  • 1944- Reject a 100% in increase from dupent, leaves behind his pregnant wife, and flies to mexico to head a new plant pathology program.over next 16 years, his team breeds 6,000 diffrent strainsof disease resitent wheat- including different varieties for each major climate on Earth.
  • 1945- discover a way to grown wheat twice each season, doubling wheat yields
  • 1953- crosses a short sturdy, dwarf breed of wheat with high yieldling American breed, creating a strain that respond well to fertilizer. it goes on providing 95% of mexico's wheat.
  • 1962- visit delhi and brings high-yielding strain of wheat to india subcontitent in time to help mitigate mass starvation due to rapid expanding population
  • 1970- recevies the nobel pecea prize
  • 1983- helps seven african countries dramatically their maize and sorghum yields
  • 1984- becomes a distinguish professor at Texas A&M University
  • 2005- states "we will have to double the world food supply by 2050." Argue that genetically modified crops are the only we can meet the demand,as we run out of arable land.Says that GM crops are not inherently dangerous because "we've been genetically modifying plant and animals for a long time. long before we called it science, people were selecting the best breeds."
  • 2009- dies at the age of 95.
